Overview

High-yield summary of all key concepts — scan before studying, review after

🔍
🦋 Structure
Butterfly-shaped; two lateral lobes + isthmus ± pyramidal lobe. Vertebral levels C5–T1. Extends from oblique line of thyroid cartilage to 5th/6th tracheal ring.
🧱 Fascial Coverings
True capsule: condensed connective tissue.
False capsule: pretracheal fascia — anchors gland to larynx. ⚠️ Exam
↕️ Swallowing Sign
Thyroid masses move upward on swallowing due to pretracheal fascia attachment. Key clinical differentiator from other neck masses. ⚠️ Exam
🫀 Arterial Supply
Superior thyroid a. — from ECA, with external laryngeal n.
Inferior thyroid a. — from thyrocervical trunk, with recurrent laryngeal n.
Thyroidea ima — 10%, from aorta/brachiocephalic
🩸 Venous Drainage
Superior + Middle → Internal jugular v.
Inferior → Brachiocephalic veins
🧬 Embryology
Thyroid: from foramen cecum → descends via thyroglossal duct → obliterates. Parathyroids: Superior from 4th pouch; Inferior from 3rd pouch. ⚠️ Exam

⚡ High-Yield Summary Cards

🔪 Surgical Ligation Rules
🩺 Superior thyroid artery
Ligate CLOSE to the gland — the external laryngeal nerve travels with the artery but diverges near the upper pole. Clamping far away risks nerve entrapment.
⚠️ Inferior thyroid artery
Ligate FAR from the gland (at its origin from thyrocervical trunk) — the recurrent laryngeal nerve interdigitates with branches near the gland. EXAM
🧬 Parathyroid Embryological Reversal
GlandPouchFinal Position
Superior parathyroid4th pouchMiddle of posterior border
Inferior parathyroid3rd pouchBase of posterior border
🔑 Why reversed?
The 3rd pouch also gives rise to the thymus — both descend together, causing the inferior parathyroids to travel farther down than their superior counterparts. Hence the embryological inversion.

⚠️ Key Exam Fact
Tracheal rings 2, 3, and 4 — "234" rule

Note: The sternomastoid and omohyoid do NOT cover the isthmus — they relate only to the lateral lobes.

Embryology

Development, descent, anomalies, and pharyngeal pouch derivatives

🧬 Thyroid Descent

👅
Week 3–4: Endodermal thickening at foramen cecum (junction of anterior 2/3 and posterior 1/3 of developing tongue)
↓
📉
Descent: Diverticulum extends downward via thyroglossal duct, passing anterior to hyoid bone and laryngeal cartilages
↓
✂️
Week 7: Thyroglossal duct normally obliterates completely — bilobed gland reaches final position (C5–T1)
↓
🔺
Remnant: Pyramidal lobe = vestigial superior extension of thyroglossal duct, sometimes tethered to hyoid by levator glandulae thyroidae

🚨 Developmental Anomalies

🫧 Thyroglossal Duct Cyst
Mechanism: Incomplete obliteration of thyroglossal duct → epithelial-lined cyst

Location: Midline, most common just inferior to hyoid bone

Hallmark sign: Elevates when patient protrudes their tongue (persistent connection to tongue base)

Complications: Infection → thyroglossal fistula

Treatment: Sistrunk procedure (remove cyst + mid-body of hyoid bone)
🌍 Ectopic Thyroid
Lingual thyroid: Failure of descent → thyroid tissue remains at foramen cecum. May be the only functional thyroid tissue!

Retrosternal/intrathoracic: Excessive descent; may compress mediastinal structures

Key rule: Before removing any ectopic thyroid, confirm functioning thyroid tissue exists elsewhere (radioiodine scan)

⚠️ The Reversal Explained
The 3rd pouch products (inferior parathyroids + thymus) migrate further caudally during development, ending up inferior to the 4th pouch products (superior parathyroids). This is why their final anatomical position is the reverse of their embryological origin.

🔬 Histological Origins (C-cells)

Parafollicular (C) Cells — Calcitonin
Derived from the ultimobranchial body (4th/5th pouch neural crest component). These cells migrate into the developing thyroid gland and become dispersed between follicles. They secrete calcitonin in response to elevated serum calcium, promoting calcium deposition into bone and reducing renal Ca²⁺ reabsorption.
MCQ Trap
C-cells are NOT derived from the foramen cecum (thyroid follicular cells) — they are neural crest derivatives via the ultimobranchial body.

Surgical Pearls

Critical points for thyroidectomy and neck surgery — all examiner favourites

🗣️
Recurrent Laryngeal Nerve
Accompanies the inferior thyroid artery near the lower pole. Injured by: ligation near gland, traction, clamping.

Result: Hoarseness (unilateral) or respiratory distress + aphonia (bilateral).

Rule: Ligate inferior thyroid artery far from gland at origin.
"اربط الانفيريور ثيرويد عند الاورجن" — ligate at origin to protect RLN
🎵
External Laryngeal Nerve
Travels with the superior thyroid artery then diverges near the upper pole. Supplies cricothyroid muscle → pitch control.

Result: Loss of high-pitched voice, monotone, vocal fatigue.

Rule: Ligate superior thyroid artery close to gland (nerve has already diverged there).
"اربط السوبيريور ثيرويد عند الجلاند" — ligate near gland to protect ELN
💉
Parathyroid Devascularization
The inferior thyroid artery is the primary blood supply to ALL 4 parathyroid glands.

Even if parathyroids are preserved anatomically, ischemic damage → functional hypoparathyroidism → hypocalcemia within 48h → tetany, Chvostek's/Trousseau's signs.
🩸
Thyroidea Ima Artery
Present in 10% of people. Arises from arch of aorta or brachiocephalic trunk. Runs anterior to trachea to supply isthmus.

Surgical danger: Emergency tracheostomy → midline pulsatile arterial bleeding inferior to isthmus. Must be anticipated!
🫁
Swallowing Test
Ask patient to swallow water while observing neck.

Moves up: Thyroid mass / thyroglossal cyst (attached via pretracheal fascia / thyroglossal duct)

Does NOT move: Lymph nodes, lipomas, other neck masses
👅
Tongue Protrusion Test
Ask patient to protrude tongue while observing neck.

Moves up with tongue: Thyroglossal duct cyst (persisting connection to foramen cecum)

Does NOT move with tongue: Thyroid mass proper
⚠️
Tracheostomy Levels
Emergency (cricothyrotomy): Through cricothyroid membrane — safest, fastest

Elective tracheostomy: Usually below isthmus (tracheal rings 3–4) or through isthmus (rings 2–3–4) after retracting it

Risk: Thyroidea ima artery if present
🧬
Intraoperative PTH Monitoring
Rapid PTH assay during parathyroid surgery. PTH half-life = 3–5 minutes. If level drops >50% after excision → confirms parathyroid adenoma removed.

Used to confirm successful parathyroidectomy in hyperparathyroidism.
🔬
Post-op Workup
For nerve injury: Nasopharyngeal laryngoscopy → vocal cord mobility

For parathyroid damage: Serum Ca²⁺ (low) + Serum PTH (low) + Serum phosphate (elevated)

Signs to look for: Chvostek's (facial tap), Trousseau's (BP cuff)
