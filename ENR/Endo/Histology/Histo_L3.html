Overview

Quick-reference summary of adrenal glands, pancreatic islets, and DNES histology

🟡

Adrenal Cortex

Three zones (G-F-R) secrete steroid hormones: Aldosterone, Cortisol, DHEA. Cells have abundant sER, tubular mitochondrial cristae, and lipid droplets. Steroids diffuse out — no granule storage.

HIGH-YIELD ⚠️ EXAM
🟤

Adrenal Medulla

Chromaffin cells (modified sympathetic neurons) arranged in cords. Store catecholamines in electron-dense granules. Epi = smaller, less dense. Norepi = larger, denser. Released by exocytosis.

HIGH-YIELD
🟢

Pancreatic Islets

1M+ islets, mostly in tail. β(60%) central=insulin, α(30%) peripheral=glucagon, δ(5-10%)=somatostatin, PP(rare)=pancreatic polypeptide. Identified by immunohistochemistry.

⚠️ EXAM CLINICAL PEARL
🔵

DNES (APUD Cells)

Scattered endocrine cells in GIT, respiratory, pancreas. Uptake amine precursors (tryptophan/tyrosine) → decarboxylate → active amines (serotonin). Act mainly via paracrine signaling.

KEY CONCEPT
⚠️
EXAM ALERT: "Steroid hormones are NOT stored inside the cell in the form of granules. They diffuse through the cell membrane to the bloodstream as they are small lipid-soluble molecules." This is a frequently tested concept.
🗺️

Structural Timeline: Adrenal Gland Layers

From capsule (outer) to medulla (inner)

🔹 Capsule

Dense irregular connective tissue with blood vessels. Sends trabeculae inward carrying vessels.

🟡 Zona Glomerulosa (15%)

Rounded/arched cords of columnar cells. Fewest lipid droplets among cortex zones. Secretes Aldosterone (mineralocorticoid).

🟡 Zona Fasciculata (65–80%)

Long, straight parallel cords 1-2 cells thick. "Spongiocytes" = massively vacuolated from dissolved lipid. Secretes Cortisol (glucocorticoid). Widest zone.

🟡 Zona Reticularis (10%)

Irregular, anastomosing branching cords. Smaller cells, less lipid, abundant lipofuscin (dark staining). Secretes weak Androgens (DHEA).

🟤 Medulla

Chromaffin cells in cords + sinusoidal capillaries + sympathetic ganglion cells. Stores catecholamines (Epi 80%, Norepi 20%) in dense granules. Reddish-brown from vascularity.

🎯

Key Histological Signs

High-yield identifiers for exam recognition

🫧 Spongiocytes EXAM
Vacuolated cells in Z. Fasciculata from dissolved lipid droplets in H&E preparation.
🟤 Lipofuscin Pigment HIGH-YIELD
Brown residual bodies in Z. Reticularis from high metabolic turnover = darker staining.
⚪ Tubular Cristae KEY CONCEPT
Unique to steroid-secreting mitochondria (not shelf-like as in protein cells). Increased enzyme surface area.
🔵 sER Abundance HIGH-YIELD
Smooth ER dominant in steroid cells (cholesterol synthesis). rER dominant in protein-secreting cells.
⚫ Chromaffin Granules EXAM
Epi = small + less dense. Norepi = large + highly dense. 150-350nm diameter. EM distinction.
🟢 β-cell Location CLINICAL
Beta cells (insulin) = 60%, centrally located. Alpha cells (glucagon) = 30%, peripheral. Identified by IHC.
💎 Silver Staining KEY CONCEPT
Argentaffin cells = stain with silver nitrate. Enterochromaffin = stain with chromium salts. For identifying DNES.
📦 No Granule Storage EXAM-FAVORITE
Steroids are lipid-soluble → diffuse directly through plasma membrane. Catecholamines/polypeptides use exocytosis.

🧭 Diagnostic Algorithm: "Unknown Endocrine Cell"

🔬 Observe cell under LM: Is it in a discrete gland or scattered in mucosa?
⬇️
If discrete gland → Is it in adrenal or pancreas?
⬇️
If adrenal, outer yellow zone → Check for vacuoles (spongiocytes = Z.Fasciculata) or dark stain (Z.Reticularis)
⬇️
If adrenal, inner reddish zone → Check EM for dense granules → Chromaffin cells (Medulla)
⬇️
If pale cluster in pancreas → Use IHC → anti-insulin (β), anti-glucagon (α), anti-somatostatin (δ)
⬇️
If scattered in GIT/respiratory mucosa → Use Silver/Chromium stains → DNES/APUD cells

📝 Model Essay Answers

Essay Q1

Describe the histological and ultrastructural (EM) features of steroid-secreting cells and explain how these features correlate with their function.

Steroid-secreting cells (e.g., in the adrenal cortex) are characterized by an acidophilic, highly vacuolated cytoplasm due to large stores of lipid droplets containing cholesterol. Ultrastructurally, they possess:
• Abundant smooth ER (sER): houses enzymes for cholesterol synthesis and initial steroid conversion steps.
• Spherical mitochondria with tubular cristae: increased surface area for enzymes catalyzing specific steroid synthesis steps + ATP production. (This contrasts with shelf-like cristae in protein-secreting cells.)
• Lipid droplets: store cholesterol precursor; dissolve during H&E prep creating the "spongiocyte" appearance (Z. Fasciculata).
• No secretory granules: steroid hormones are small, lipid-soluble molecules that diffuse directly through the plasma membrane into adjacent fenestrated sinusoidal capillaries.
These features collectively optimize continuous steroid production and immediate release.

Essay Q2

Compare and contrast the three zones of the adrenal cortex regarding their cellular arrangement, lipid content, and secreted hormones.

Zona Glomerulosa (15%): Rounded/arched cords of columnar/pyramidal cells beneath the capsule. Moderate lipid content. Secretes mineralocorticoids (primarily aldosterone).

Zona Fasciculata (65-80%): Long, straight, parallel cords 1-2 cells thick. Highest lipid content — cells appear as "spongiocytes" due to dissolved lipid vacuoles. Secretes glucocorticoids (primarily cortisol).

Zona Reticularis (10%): Smaller cells in irregular, anastomosing branching cords. Lowest lipid content but high lipofuscin pigment (from metabolic aging), giving darker H&E staining. Secretes weak androgens (DHEA) and some cortisol.

All three zones share the ultrastructural features of steroid-secreting cells (sER, tubular mitochondrial cristae), but differ in cell size, arrangement, lipid storage, and hormonal product.
