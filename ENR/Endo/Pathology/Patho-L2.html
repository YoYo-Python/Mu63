🧠 Pituitary Gland

Master gland controlling growth, reproduction, and metabolism. Key pathology: adenomas causing hyper/hypopituitarism and mass effects.

⚠️ EXAM: Benign vs malignant endocrine tumors — only metastasis differentiates them microscopically!

🔺 Adrenal Gland

Cortex produces cortisol, aldosterone, androgens. Medulla produces catecholamines. Key pathologies: Cushing's, Conn's, Pheochromocytoma, Neuroblastoma.

⚠️ EXAM: Cushing's Syndrome ≠ Cushing's Disease. Disease = pituitary ACTH adenoma only!

🫀 Endocrine Pancreas

Islets of Langerhans produce insulin, glucagon, somatostatin. Key pathology: Diabetes Mellitus (Type 1 vs 2), Islet cell tumors.

⚠️ EXAM: Type 2 DM is NOT lack of insulin — it's resistance! Insulin may be normal or increased.

Disease Progression Overview

🔬 Pituitary Adenoma Formation

Uncontrolled proliferation of a single anterior pituitary cell type → monomorphic cellular mass → loss of reticulin network → microadenoma (<1cm) → macroadenoma (>1cm) with mass effects.

⚡ Adrenal Cortex Hyperfunction

ACTH-secreting pituitary adenoma → bilateral adrenal hyperplasia → excess cortisol → Cushing's Disease. OR autonomous adrenal adenoma → Cushing's Syndrome with contralateral atrophy.

🩸 Diabetic Complications

Chronic hyperglycemia → non-enzymatic glycosylation → capillary basement membrane thickening (microangiopathy) → retinopathy, nephropathy (Kimmelstiel-Wilson), neuropathy. Accelerated atherosclerosis → macroangiopathy.

🎯 Pheochromocytoma Presentation

Chromaffin cell tumor → autonomous catecholamine secretion → paroxysmal hypertension, sweating, headache → VMA elevated in urine. "10% rule" applies.

Essay Questions & Model Answers

Q1: Explain the mechanisms by which a pituitary macroadenoma produces clinical symptoms, including both endocrine and local mass effects.

▼
A pituitary macroadenoma produces endocrine symptoms either by over-secreting a specific hormone (e.g., prolactin causing galactorrhea, GH causing acromegaly) or by compressing normal adjacent pituitary tissue, causing hypopituitarism (decreased normal hormone output). Local mass effects occur because the tumor is situated in the sella turcica; as it grows, it compresses the optic chiasm above it (causing bitemporal hemianopia) and raises intracranial pressure, leading to headaches. Spontaneous hemorrhage into the mass can cause acute pituitary apoplexy.

Q2: Contrast the pathogenesis and clinical manifestations of functional adenomas secreting Growth Hormone based on patient's age of onset.

▼
Both Gigantism and Acromegaly are caused by a functional pituitary adenoma comprising granular acidophilic cells that secrete excess Growth Hormone (GH). The mechanism involves GH stimulating the liver to overproduce IGF-I. The distinction is entirely dependent on the patient's age (whether the bone epiphyses have fused). If the adenoma occurs in children before epiphyseal closure, the long bones grow excessively, causing Gigantism. If it occurs in adults after closure, bones grow in thickness (jaw, hands, frontal bossing), resulting in Acromegaly. Both present with secondary issues like diabetes, hypertension, and heart failure.

Q3: Discuss the pathophysiological mechanisms differentiating primary from secondary hyperaldosteronism.

▼
Primary hyperaldosteronism (Conn's Syndrome) is caused by an intrinsic adrenal defect, most commonly (80%) a unilateral adrenal adenoma or bilateral primary adrenal hyperplasia. The autonomous secretion of aldosterone suppresses renin. Secondary hyperaldosteronism is an extrinsic response; decreased renal perfusion triggers the kidneys to secrete renin, activating the renin-angiotensin system, which subsequently stimulates the normal adrenal cortex to produce aldosterone. Both result in sodium retention, potassium excretion (hypokalemia), and hypertension.

Q4: Describe the pathogenesis, origin, and the "10% rule" characteristics of pheochromocytoma.

▼
Pheochromocytoma is a tumor of the adrenal medulla composed of chromaffin cells and nerve endings. It autonomously secretes catecholamines (epinephrine, norepinephrine), which are metabolized into VMA and excreted in urine. Excess catecholamines cause paroxysmal or sustained hypertension. It follows the "10% rule": 10% are bilateral, 10% are extra-adrenal (paragangliomas), 10% are familial (associated with MEN syndromes), and 10% are malignant. Malignancy can only be proven by the presence of metastases.

Q5: Outline the mechanisms of vascular and renal complications arising from chronic uncompensated hyperglycemia.

▼
Chronic uncompensated hyperglycemia causes widespread vascular damage known as diabetic microangiopathy. The mechanism involves diffuse thickening of the capillary basement membranes in the skin, retina, peripheral nerves, and renal medulla, leading to "leaky vessels." In the kidney, this manifests as diabetic nephropathy, specifically causing capillary BM thickening, nodular glomerulosclerosis (Kimmelstiel-Wilson lesions in 15-30%), and diffuse mesangial sclerosis. In large vessels, it accelerates atherosclerosis, increasing the risk of cardiovascular and CNS complications.

Q6: Correlate the functional properties of islet cell tumors (Insulinoma, Gastrinoma) with their distinct clinical presentations.

▼
An Insulinoma arises from beta cells and secretes unregulated insulin. Because insulin lowers blood glucose, patients present with severe hypoglycemic attacks (<50 mg/dl) precipitated by fasting and relieved by eating. In contrast, a Gastrinoma secretes excess gastrin. Gastrin strongly stimulates the stomach to produce gastric acid. This massive hyperacidity overcomes intestinal neutralization, leading to Zollinger-Ellison Syndrome, characterized by intractable, multiple peptic ulcers (often in the jejunum) and diarrhea.
