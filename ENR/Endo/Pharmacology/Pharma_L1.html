🧪 Diagnosis & Monitoring

TSH is the compass for every decision here — interpret it correctly before you touch a dose.

TSH interpretation

↑ High TSH → Hypothyroidism (pituitary straining to drive a failing gland) ↓ Low TSH → Hyperthyroidism (excess hormone suppresses pituitary)

Diagnostic / decision algorithm — suspected thyroid storm

1

Recognize the picture clinically

Fever ≥ 40°C, severe tachycardia, confusion in a patient with known/untreated Graves' disease — diagnosis is clinical, don't wait for labs to start treating.

2

Confirm with labs (don't delay treatment)

TSH will be severely suppressed; free T3/T4 exceptionally high.

3

Start the 4-drug protocol immediately

Propranolol → Thioamide → Iodide → Corticosteroid (see Treatment tab).

4

Reassess and plan definitive therapy

Once stabilized, plan RAI ablation or surgery for definitive long-term control.

💊 Treatment

Color-coded by urgency: red for the emergency room, green for lifelong maintenance.

🚨 Acute / Emergency — Thyroid Storm

  • Propranolol — beta-blockade + ↓ peripheral T4→T3 conversion
  • PTU / Methimazole — halts new hormone synthesis (TPO inhibition)
  • Iodides (Lugol's) — rapidly blocks hormone release
  • Corticosteroids — ↓ T4→T3 conversion + anti-inflammatory

🟢 Chronic / Maintenance

  • Methimazole — first-line for mild Graves', young patients
  • Levothyroxine — lifelong replacement, daily, empty stomach
  • Radioactive Iodine — definitive cure, age >35, not pregnant
  • Diltiazem — heart-rate control when beta-blockers contraindicated
