Overview
High-yield summary of the Hypothalamic–Pituitary axis — every concept you need to dominate the exam.
⚠️
EXAM BOMB: ADH binds to V2 receptors in the DCT and collecting ducts. Via adenylate cyclase/cAMP it induces insertion of Aquaporin-2 channels → dilutes plasma, concentrates urine. A mere 1–2% rise in plasma osmolarity is enough to trigger this.
⚠️
EXAM BOMB: GH has no direct growth-promoting effects. All growth effects are mediated by Somatomedins (IGF-1) produced in the liver.
⚠️
EXAM BOMB: The endocrine system regulates metabolism, growth, reproduction, and homeostasis. Hormones circulate systemically but act only on target organs with specific receptors.
Signaling Types
Hormonal Signaling Patterns
Endocrine: Into bloodstream → distant target organs.
Paracrine: Into ECF → neighboring cells only.
Autocrine: Acts on the same cell that secreted it.
Chemistry
Chemical Nature of Hormones
Protein/Peptide: Pituitary, Hypothalamus, Calcitonin, Parathyroid hormones.
Steroid (from cholesterol): Adrenal cortical, Sex hormones, Vitamin D3.
Exam Favorite
Hormone Receptor Locations
Cell membrane: Protein/peptide hormones + catecholamines.
Cytoplasmic: Steroid hormones.
Nuclear: Thyroid hormones.
Posterior Pit.
Posterior Pituitary — Structure
Neural tissue, a downgrowth from the 3rd ventricle. Connected to hypothalamus via the hypothalamic–hypophyseal tract. Does NOT synthesize hormones — it stores and releases them.
⚠️ High Yield
ADH / Vasopressin
Synthesized: Supraoptic nucleus.
V2 → cAMP → Aquaporin-2 → water reabsorption in DCT & collecting ducts.
V1 (large doses) → vasoconstriction.
Regulated by: plasma osmolarity (primary) & blood volume.
Mechanism
Oxytocin
Synthesized: Paraventricular nucleus.
Actions: milk ejection (myoepithelial cell contraction via suckling) + uterine smooth muscle contraction in labor (positive feedback).
Anterior Pit.
Anterior Pituitary — Control
No direct neural contact with hypothalamus. Controlled via hypophyseal portal system.
Releasing hormones: TRH, CRH, GHRH, GnRH, PRH.
Inhibiting: Somatostatin (GHIH), Dopamine (PIH).
⚠️ High Yield
Growth Hormone (GH)
Secreted by: Somatotropes (acidophils).
Growth via: IGF-1 (liver) → epiphyseal cartilage proliferation.
Metabolic: Protein anabolic ↑, Lipolysis ↑, Glucose ↑ (anti-insulin).
Prolactin
Prolactin
Secreted by: Mammotropes (acidophils).
Function: breast development, milk synthesis (proteins, fat, lactose).
High levels → inhibit ovulation (amenorrhea).
Regulated primarily by dopamine (PIH) — tonic inhibition.
Clinical
Diabetes Insipidus
Neurogenic: Absent ADH (supraoptic nucleus damage).
Nephrogenic: Renal unresponsiveness to ADH (V2/Aquaporin-2 failure).
Result: Urine up to 20 L/day, low specific gravity, polydipsia.
Clinical
SIADH
Inappropriate continuous ADH secretion → excessive water retention → plasma dilution → hyponatremia + concentrated urine despite increased plasma volume.
Clinical
Acromegaly vs. Gigantism
Acromegaly: Excess GH in adults (after epiphyseal union) → bone thickening, prognathism, enlarged extremities, hyperglycemia.
Gigantism: Excess GH in children (open epiphyses) → linear bone overgrowth.
Clinical
Hyperprolactinemia
Female: Galactorrhea + amenorrhea (inhibits FSH/LH → no ovulation).
Male: Galactorrhea + hypogonadism + impotence.
Cause: Mammotrope adenoma or loss of dopamine inhibition.
Clinical
Panhypopituitarism / Simmonds
Complete destruction of anterior pituitary (e.g., post-partum hemorrhage → Sheehan syndrome) → loss of all trophic hormones → atrophy of thyroid, adrenal, gonads → weight loss, failure of lactation.
GH Deficiency
Pituitary Dwarfism
GH deficiency in childhood → symmetrical, proportionate growth retardation. Normal mental and sexual development. Hypoglycemia (opposite of GH excess). Compare: Achondroplasia has disproportionate limb shortening.
Cell Types
Anterior Pituitary Cell Types
🟡 Thyrotropes → TSH
🟠 Corticotropes → ACTH
🟣 Gonadotropes → FSH & LH
🔵 Somatotropes → GH
🟢 Mammotropes → Prolactin
Clinical Pearl
Bitemporal Hemianopia
Pituitary tumor (especially macroadenoma) grows upward, compressing the optic chiasma → loss of nasal retinal fibers from both eyes → bitemporal visual field loss (tunnel vision).

Key Structures — Click to Explore

🧠
Hypothalamus
Master control center. Contains supraoptic nucleus (ADH) and paraventricular nucleus (Oxytocin). Releases hormones via median eminence into the portal system to control anterior pituitary.
🔵
Supraoptic Nucleus
Site of ADH (vasopressin) synthesis. Osmoreceptors here respond to plasma osmolarity changes as small as 1–2%. Projects axons down the hypothalamic–hypophyseal tract to posterior pituitary.
🟣
Paraventricular Nucleus
Primary site of Oxytocin synthesis. Also produces some ADH. Responds to suckling signals (milk ejection) and labor signals (uterine contraction). Positive feedback loops govern both.
🔴
Posterior Pituitary
Neurohypophysis. A neural downgrowth from the 3rd ventricle. Stores and releases ADH and Oxytocin from axon terminals — it does NOT synthesize these hormones itself.
🟡
Anterior Pituitary
Adenohypophysis. Glandular tissue. No direct neural link to hypothalamus — controlled exclusively via the hypophyseal portal blood system. Contains somatotropes, mammotropes, corticotropes, thyrotropes, gonadotropes.
💧
Portal System
Hypophyseal portal system: capillaries drain the median eminence of the hypothalamus and reform into sinusoids in the anterior pituitary. Delivers releasing/inhibiting hormones in high concentration directly to pituitary cells.
🫘
V2 Receptor / Aquaporin-2
V2 receptors on DCT and collecting duct cells. ADH binding → Gs → adenylate cyclase → cAMP → PKA → Aquaporin-2 vesicles fuse with apical membrane → water permeability increases dramatically → water reabsorption.
📈
IGF-1 / Somatomedins
Produced primarily by the liver in response to GH. Mediates ALL growth-promoting effects of GH including: epiphyseal cartilage proliferation, protein synthesis, and organ growth. High IGF-1 feeds back to suppress GHRH and stimulate Somatostatin.
👁️
Optic Chiasma
Lies directly above the pituitary gland. Nasal retinal fibers from both eyes cross here. A pituitary macroadenoma expanding superiorly compresses the chiasma → bitemporal hemianopia (loss of peripheral/temporal visual fields).

⚙️ ADH Action Flowchart — Click steps to expand

Trigger
↑ Plasma Osmolarity (≥1–2% above normal) OR ↓ Blood Volume (≥5–10%)
Osmoreceptors in the supraoptic nucleus detect plasma hyperosmolarity. Baroreceptors in carotid sinus/aortic arch detect hypotension. Both signals converge on the hypothalamus.
Synthesis & Release
Supraoptic nucleus synthesizes ADH → travels down hypothalamic–hypophyseal tract → released from posterior pituitary
ADH is synthesized as a large precursor (prepro-ADH) in the cell bodies of magnocellular neurons in the supraoptic nucleus. It is packaged into vesicles and transported axonally to the pposterior pituitary nerve terminals where it awaits calcium-triggered exocytosis.
Receptor Binding
ADH binds V2 receptors on the basolateral side of DCT & collecting duct cells
V2 receptors are G-protein coupled receptors (Gs). They are located on the basolateral membrane of principal cells in the distal convoluted tubule and collecting duct of the kidney.
Second Messenger
Gs → Adenylate Cyclase activation → ↑ cAMP → Protein Kinase A activation
The Gs subunit activates adenylate cyclase, converting ATP to cyclic AMP (cAMP). cAMP activates PKA (Protein Kinase A), which phosphorylates target proteins including Aquaporin-2 vesicle-docking machinery.
Aquaporin-2 Insertion
PKA phosphorylates → Aquaporin-2 vesicles fuse with apical membrane
Aquaporin-2 water channels are pre-synthesized and stored in intracellular vesicles. PKA phosphorylation triggers vesicle fusion with the apical (luminal) membrane, inserting functional water channels. The basolateral membranes already constitutively express AQP3 and AQP4.
Outcome
Water moves from tubular lumen → cell → peritubular capillaries → blood
The hyperosmotic medullary interstitium creates an osmotic gradient that pulls water out of the tubule through the newly inserted Aquaporin-2 channels. Result: concentrated urine (↑ specific gravity, ↑ osmolarity) and diluted plasma (↓ osmolarity, ↑ blood volume).

📈 GH → IGF-1 → Growth Flowchart

Stimulus
Hypoglycemia / Starvation / Deep Sleep / Stress / Exercise / GHRH from hypothalamus
The strongest stimuli for GH secretion are hypoglycemia and protein/calorie deprivation (starvation). GH secretion follows a pulsatile pattern with the largest peak occurring 60–90 minutes after sleep onset (slow-wave sleep). GHRH from the hypothalamus via portal blood stimulates somatotropes.
GH Secretion
Somatotropes (acidophils) in anterior pituitary release Growth Hormone
Somatotropes constitute ~50% of anterior pituitary cells. They are the most abundant cell type. GH is a 191-amino acid single-chain polypeptide. GH secretion is inhibited by Somatostatin (GHIH) from the hypothalamus and by high IGF-1 (negative feedback).
Liver Action
GH binds receptors in liver → stimulates Somatomedin (IGF-1) synthesis
The liver is the major source of circulating IGF-1 (Insulin-like Growth Factor 1). GH binds to JAK2-associated receptors on hepatocytes, triggering STAT5 signaling and transcription of IGF-1 gene. IGF-1 has structural similarity to proinsulin.
Growth Effects (via IGF-1)
IGF-1 → epiphyseal cartilage proliferation → linear bone growth (children) OR periosteal thickening (adults)
In children with open epiphyseal plates: IGF-1 stimulates chondrocyte proliferation in the growth plate → linear height increase (Gigantism if excess). In adults with fused epiphyses: bones can only thicken (periosteal apposition) → Acromegaly features: prognathism, enlarged hands/feet/skull.
Metabolic Effects (direct GH)
↑ Protein synthesis, ↑ Lipolysis (free fatty acids), ↑ Blood glucose (anti-insulin)
GH is a counter-regulatory (anti-insulin) hormone: (1) Decreases glucose uptake by muscle and fat. (2) Increases hepatic gluconeogenesis. (3) Stimulates lipolysis → ↑ free fatty acids as energy source. (4) Promotes protein synthesis (anabolic effect on muscle). Chronic GH excess → Diabetes Mellitus.
Negative Feedback
High IGF-1 → ↓ GHRH + ↑ Somatostatin → ↓ GH secretion
IGF-1 exerts negative feedback at both the hypothalamus (stimulates Somatostatin release, inhibits GHRH) and directly on somatotropes in the anterior pituitary. A GH-secreting adenoma may be RESISTANT to this feedback, maintaining autonomous hypersecretion.

Diagnostic Algorithm — Acromegaly

1
Clinical Suspicion
Enlarged hands/feet, prognathism, coarsening of facial features, increased hat/shoe/ring size, headaches, visual field defects, hyperglycemia.
PrognathismAcral enlargementBitemporal hemianopiaHyperglycemia
2
Screening: IGF-1 level
Elevated serum IGF-1 (age- and sex-matched) is the best screening test. IGF-1 reflects integrated 24-hr GH secretion (avoids pulsatility problem of random GH).
3
Confirmation: Oral Glucose Suppression Test (OGTT)
Administer 75g oral glucose. Normally GH falls to <1 ng/mL. In Acromegaly, GH remains elevated or paradoxically rises (no suppression). This is confirmatory.
4
Imaging: MRI of Pituitary
MRI with gadolinium contrast to localize and size the adenoma. Macroadenoma (>10mm) is more likely to compress optic chiasma. Microadenoma (<10mm) has fewer mass effects.
Macroadenoma >10mmMicroadenoma <10mmSuprasellar extension
5
Assess Complications
Fasting blood glucose (diabetes), visual field testing (perimetry for bitemporal hemianopia), echocardiogram (cardiac enlargement), colonoscopy (GH increases colorectal cancer risk).

Treatment 💊
Management principles for pituitary disorders — color-coded by urgency and category.

🚨 Pituitary Apoplexy (Emergency)

🚨 Emergency
Acute Pituitary Apoplexy
  • Immediate IV hydrocortisone (adrenal crisis risk)
  • Fluid resuscitation and electrolyte correction
  • Urgent neurosurgical referral if visual compromise or declining consciousness
  • Decompressive surgery (transsphenoidal) if indicated

Acromegaly Treatment

✅ First Line
Transsphenoidal Surgery
  • Surgical resection of pituitary adenoma
  • Curative in 80–85% of microadenomas
  • Immediate reduction of GH levels
  • Relieves optic chiasma compression
⚕️ Medical
Somatostatin Analogues
  • Octreotide, Lanreotide — inhibit GH secretion
  • Used pre-operatively or in surgical failures
  • GH receptor antagonist: Pegvisomant
  • Dopamine agonists (Bromocriptine): mild cases
🔭 Adjuvant
Radiation Therapy
  • Stereotactic radiosurgery (Gamma Knife)
  • Used when surgery incomplete and medical therapy fails
  • GH normalization takes months to years
  • Risk: hypopituitarism (pan-hormonal deficiency)

Diabetes Insipidus Treatment

✅ Neurogenic DI
DDAVP (Desmopressin)
  • Synthetic ADH analogue (V2-selective)
  • Given intranasally, orally, or IV
  • Dramatically reduces urine volume
  • Must monitor for hyponatremia (over-treatment)
⚕️ Nephrogenic DI
Thiazide Diuretics + Low-Salt Diet
  • Thiazides → mild volume depletion → ↑ proximal tubule reabsorption → less water delivered distally
  • Low-sodium diet reduces obligatory free water loss
  • NSAIDs (e.g., Indomethacin) as adjunct
  • DDAVP is ineffective (receptor defect)
💧 Supportive
Fluid Replacement
  • Match fluid intake to output (oral hydration)
  • IV hypotonic fluids if unable to drink
  • Monitor sodium levels closely
  • Avoid rapid correction of hypernatremia (cerebral edema risk)

SIADH Treatment

✅ First Line
Fluid Restriction
  • Restrict fluid intake to 500–1000 mL/day
  • Allows gradual rise in plasma Na⁺
  • Correct underlying cause (treat head injury, remove offending drug, treat ectopic ADH source)
⚕️ Pharmacological
Vaptans / Hypertonic Saline
  • Vaptans (Tolvaptan, Conivaptan): V2 receptor antagonists — "aquaretics" — free water excretion without Na⁺ loss
  • Hypertonic saline (3% NaCl): for severe symptomatic hyponatremia — correct Na⁺ slowly (<10 mEq/L per 24h to avoid central pontine myelinolysis)
  • Demeclocycline: induces nephrogenic DI (historical)

Hyperprolactinemia Treatment

✅ First Line
Dopamine Agonists
  • Cabergoline (preferred — once/twice weekly, more tolerable)
  • Bromocriptine (older, more side effects — nausea, hypotension)
  • Mimic dopamine's tonic inhibition of prolactin
  • Shrinks adenoma in most cases; restores ovulation
⚕️ Surgical
Transsphenoidal Surgery
  • Reserved for drug-resistant cases
  • Or when visual fields are compromised
  • Less effective for macroadenomas than for GH adenomas

Panhypopituitarism / Hormone Replacement

✅ Replacement Therapy
Hormone Substitution
  • Hydrocortisone → cortisol (adrenal axis, priority #1)
  • Levothyroxine → thyroid hormone (start after cortisol)
  • Sex steroids → testosterone (M) or estrogen/progesterone (F)
  • GH replacement in adults (quality of life, body composition)
  • ADH (DDAVP) if posterior pituitary also affected
