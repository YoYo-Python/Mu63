⚡ The Emergency Hormone Factory

The adrenal medulla is a modified sympathetic ganglion — it has no postganglionic axons. Instead, it converts neural signals into a hormonal storm of epinephrine and norepinephrine, bridging the nervous and endocrine systems in milliseconds.

🏗️ Anatomy & Histology
  • Modified sympathetic ganglion — NO postganglionic axons
  • Innervated by long preganglionic cholinergic neurons
  • Fibers originate from lateral horn cells T5-T9
  • Travel via the greater splanchnic nerve
  • Synapse directly with chromaffin cells (pheochromocytes)
  • Acts as a neuroendocrine transducer
⚗️ Synthesis & Metabolism
  • Precursor: L-Tyrosine → enzymatic cascade → catecholamines
  • Stored in chromaffin granules
  • Neuronal inactivation → MAO (mitochondrial)
  • Extraneuronal inactivation → COMT (liver, kidney, lung, gut)
  • Final urinary product: VMA (vanillyl mandelic acid)
  • Half-life only 2–3 minutes
🎛️ Regulation of Secretion
  • Controlled by A.S.C in medulla oblongata
  • Normal BP → baroreceptors send inhibitory impulses to A.S.C
  • Hemorrhage → removes inhibition → A.S.C fires → catecholamine surge
  • Stimulated by: pain, emotions, cold, hypoglycemia, hypoxia
  • Secretion ↓ during sleep
  • Needs cortisol (HPA axis) for catecholamine synthesis
⚠️ EXAM TRAP: Baroreceptors during hemorrhage are NOT "inhibited" — their normal inhibitory input is REMOVED, releasing the A.S.C.
❤️ CVS Actions
  • ↑ Heart rate, force, O₂ consumption
  • Vasoconstriction everywhere EXCEPT skeletal muscle & liver
  • Skeletal muscle & liver: vasodilation via β2
  • Renal: vasoconstriction → ↓ GFR → ↑ renin (β1) → aldosterone → Na⁺ reabsorption (distal tubule)
  • Skin: pallor due to α vasoconstriction
⚠️ EXAM: Na⁺ reabsorption from aldosterone occurs at DISTAL tubules, not proximal!
🔥 Metabolic Actions
  • Hyperglycemia: ↑ glycogenolysis + blocks insulin + ↑ glucagon + ↑ ACTH/cortisol
  • Suppresses glucose transporter proteins in skeletal muscle
  • Lipolysis → Free fatty acids (fuel for resting muscle)
  • ↑ BMR, ↑ O₂ consumption
  • Dual pancreatic effect: β → ↑ insulin/glucagon; α → ↓ insulin/glucagon
🎯 Pheochromocytoma
  • Tumor of chromaffin cells → hypersecretion of catecholamines
  • Urine catecholamines >300 µg/day (normal 10–100 µg/day)
  • Symptoms: hypertension, tachycardia, sweating, headache, pallor, hyperglycemia, pupillary dilation
  • Diagnosis: urinary VMA measurement
  • Elevated BP can NOT suppress tumor (autonomous source)

💊 Management
🚨 Pheochromocytoma — Emergency Management

α-Blockers First

Phenoxybenzamine (irreversible α-blocker) to control hypertension BEFORE surgery. Never give β-blocker first (risk of unopposed α = hypertensive crisis).

Then β-Blockers

Added after α-blockade to control tachycardia. Propranolol often used. Order matters — α then β.

Surgical Resection

Definitive treatment. Laparoscopic adrenalectomy. Pre-operative α/β blockade for 2 weeks mandatory.

💉 Catecholamine Pharmacology Targets

MAO Inhibitors

Block neuronal catecholamine breakdown → prolonged action. Risk: hypertensive crisis with tyramine-rich foods (cheese reaction).

COMT Inhibitors

Used in Parkinson's disease adjunct therapy to prolong L-DOPA activity. Entacapone, Tolcapone.

Beta-2 Agonists

Mimic vasodilation effect in skeletal muscle & bronchodilation. Salbutamol for asthma. Also used in preterm labor (tocolysis).

✅ Adrenalectomy — Post-op Note

After bilateral adrenalectomy, NO epinephrine replacement is needed for the medullary component. The adrenal medulla is not essential for life because sympathetic nerve endings throughout the body produce sufficient catecholamines for normal biologic requirements. Adrenocortical replacement (cortisol + aldosterone) IS required.

⚠️ EXAM POINT: Hyposecretion of adrenal medulla = asymptomatic. No replacement needed. (Unlike adrenal cortex!)
