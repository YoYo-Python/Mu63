The Adrenal Cortex

Physiology · Structure · Hormones · Regulation

🌊 Zona Glomerulosa

Mineralocorticoids → Aldosterone

  • Outermost layer
  • Regulated by RAAS & hyperkalemia
  • Reabsorbs Na⁺, excretes K⁺ & H⁺
  • Controls blood volume & pressure
  • NOT primarily ACTH-controlled
Mineralocorticoid RAAS

☀️ Zona Fasciculata

Glucocorticoids → Cortisol

  • Middle (largest) layer
  • Regulated exclusively by ACTH
  • Metabolic regulation, stress response
  • Anti-inflammatory powerhouse
  • Circadian rhythm: peak 4-10 AM
Glucocorticoid High-Yield

🔴 Zona Reticularis

Androgens → DHEA & Androstenedione

  • Innermost layer
  • Shares ACTH control with fasciculata
  • Considered one functional zone
  • Weak androgens
  • Clinically less significant
Androgens ACTH-controlled

🕐 Circadian Cortisol Rhythm

4 AM – 10 AM

Peak cortisol levels (highest in the day). Just before waking. Prepares body for daily stress.

Midday

Cortisol declining from peak. Still metabolically active.

~Midnight

Lowest cortisol levels (nadir). Important diagnostic reference point.

Acute Stress (Any Time)

ACTH surge → cortisol spikes regardless of circadian phase. Overrides normal rhythm.

🔥 Anti-inflammatory Mechanism

Multiple pathways by which cortisol suppresses inflammation

1️⃣ Stabilizes lysosomal membranes → prevents proteolytic enzyme release
2️⃣ Decreases capillary permeability → less edema
3️⃣ Reduces WBC migration to inflamed sites
4️⃣ Suppresses T-lymphocyte reproduction
5️⃣ Reduces IL-1 release → lowers fever
6️⃣ Inhibits histamine from mast cells & basophils (anti-allergic)

🍬 Glucose Metabolism

How cortisol causes hyperglycemia

↑ Extrahepatic gluconeogenesis from mobilized amino acids
↓ Cellular glucose utilization (insulin antagonism)
Net effect: ↑ Plasma glucose
Source: extrahepatic protein catabolism
→ Diabetogenic at high levels (steroid diabetes)

🥩 Protein Metabolism

Dual anabolic/catabolic protein effects

Extrahepatic: CATABOLIC — reduces cellular protein stores, mobilizes amino acids
Hepatic: ANABOLIC — increases liver protein synthesis & plasma proteins
Net: ↑ Amino acid supply to liver for gluconeogenesis
Clinical: muscle wasting, osteoporosis, thin skin, striae

🫙 Fat Metabolism

Lipolysis and fat redistribution

↑ Lipolysis → mobilizes fatty acids from adipose tissue
Shifts cellular fuel from glucose to fatty acids
Fat redistributes: limbs lose fat → central/facial accumulation
Clinical: buffalo hump, moon face, central obesity in Cushing's

💉 Blood Cell Effects

Cortisol's hematopoietic actions

↑ RBCs → polycythemia (redness of moon face)
↑ Platelets (thrombocytosis)
↑ Neutrophils (stimulates hematopoiesis)
↓ Lymphocytes → migrate to lymphoid tissue
↓ Eosinophils & basophils → migrate out of circulation

💪 Permissive Action

Cortisol's role enabling other hormones

Cortisol must be present for:
• Glucagon to produce gluconeogenesis
• Catecholamines to cause lipolysis
• Catecholamines to cause bronchodilation
• Catecholamines to exert vasopressor responses
→ Essential for stress survival

🫀 Vascular Reactivity

How cortisol maintains blood pressure

↑ Expression of α₁ receptors on arterioles
↑ Sensitivity to norepinephrine vasoconstrictor effect
Without cortisol: blood vessels lose responsiveness to catecholamines
This is why Addison's → hypotension + vasodilatory shock

🌊 Renal Cortisol Effect

Antidiuretic effect of cortisol

Cortisol has weak mineralocorticoid activity
Enhances Na⁺ reabsorption from renal tubules
Obligatory water reabsorption follows Na⁺
In dehydration: antidiuretic effect secondary to enhanced Na⁺ reabsorption
Both ADH + cortisol absence → excessive water loss

⚠️ Cushing's Syndrome Cascade

Excess Cortisol (Primary / Secondary / Iatrogenic)
↓
🦴 ↑Protein catabolism → Osteoporosis, striae, muscle wasting
🍬 ↑Gluconeogenesis → Steroid diabetes, hyperglycemia

🔬 Investigation Findings

Conn's Syndrome

↑ Aldosterone ↓ Renin ↓ K⁺ ↑ Na⁺ Alkalosis pH

CT abdomen to identify adrenal adenoma. Aldosterone:renin ratio ↑↑.

Cushing's Syndrome

↑ Cortisol Loss of diurnal rhythm ↑ ACTH (pituitary) ↑ Glucose ↑ RBCs

24h urinary cortisol ↑. Dexamethasone suppression test: failed suppression. MRI pituitary or CT adrenal.

Addison's Disease

↓ Cortisol ↑ ACTH ↓ Na⁺ ↑ K⁺ ↓ Glucose

Short synacthen test: cortisol fails to rise. ACTH stimulation test. Anti-adrenal antibodies if autoimmune.

🗺️ Diagnostic Algorithm — Suspected Cushing's

1

Clinical Suspicion

Moon face, central obesity, striae, hypertension, hyperglycemia, muscle wasting

2

24-Hour Urinary Cortisol

First-line screening. Loss of normal diurnal rhythm is key. Normal: peak at 4-10 AM, nadir at midnight.

3

Plasma ACTH Level

↑ ACTH → pituitary-driven (secondary Cushing's / Cushing's disease). ↓ ACTH → adrenal tumor (primary).

4

Dexamethasone Suppression Test

Give exogenous steroid → normal response: cortisol suppressed. Cushing's: cortisol NOT suppressed.

5

Imaging

MRI pituitary (if ACTH-dependent) or CT adrenal (if adrenal-dependent). Look for adenoma or hyperplasia.

6

Differentiate Source

Adrenal tumor → primary. Pituitary ACTH tumor → secondary/Cushing's disease. Exogenous steroids → iatrogenic.

🚨 Diagnostic Algorithm — Addisonian Crisis

1

Trigger Recognition

Patient with known Addison's exposed to acute stress (infection, surgery, trauma)

2

Clinical Features

Vascular collapse (shock), severe hypotension, nausea, vomiting, extreme weakness, confusion

3

Emergency Labs

↓ Cortisol, ↑ ACTH, ↓ Na⁺, ↑ K⁺, ↓ glucose. Do NOT delay treatment for lab results.

4

Immediate Treatment

IV hydrocortisone + IV saline + glucose. Life-saving — cannot wait for confirmation.

💊 Treatment

Emergency protocols, drugs, and management by disorder.

🚨 Addisonian Crisis — Emergency

⚡ IMMEDIATE ACTION REQUIRED

Patient in vascular collapse after stress exposure (infection, surgery, illness). Do NOT delay treatment for labs.

IV Hydrocortisone
100 mg IV bolus immediately, then 50-100 mg every 6h. Replaces missing cortisol and aldosterone.
IV Normal Saline
Aggressive fluid resuscitation for hypovolemia/shock. Corrects Na⁺ deficit.
IV Dextrose
Corrects hypoglycemia. Give 50% dextrose if glucose very low.
Treat Precipitant
Address underlying stress trigger (antibiotics for infection, etc.)

🔴 Addison's Disease — Chronic Management

Hydrocortisone
15-25 mg/day in divided doses. Replaces cortisol. Give higher dose at morning (mimics circadian peak).
Fludrocortisone
0.05-0.2 mg/day. Mineralocorticoid replacement. Reduces Na⁺ loss, prevents hyperkalemia.
Sick Day Rules
Double/triple steroid dose during illness or surgery. Patient education critical.
Medical Alert Bracelet
Mandatory. Essential for emergency care providers to know about the condition.

🟡 Cushing's Syndrome — Treatment

Surgical Removal
First-line for adrenal adenoma (adrenalectomy) or pituitary adenoma (transsphenoidal surgery).
Metyrapone
Inhibits cortisol synthesis (11β-hydroxylase inhibitor). Used pre-operatively or when surgery not possible.
Ketoconazole
Inhibits multiple steroidogenic enzymes. Used for medical management.
Cabergoline
Dopamine agonist — reduces ACTH in Cushing's disease (pituitary source).
Treat Iatrogenic Cause
Gradual steroid taper if caused by exogenous steroids. Do NOT stop suddenly (risk of Addisonian crisis).

🔵 Conn's Syndrome — Treatment

Adrenalectomy
First-line if unilateral adenoma confirmed on imaging. Curative.
Spironolactone
Aldosterone antagonist. Used for bilateral hyperplasia or pre-surgery. Corrects hypokalemia and HTN.
Eplerenone
Selective mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist. Fewer anti-androgenic side effects than spironolactone.
Potassium Supplementation
Correct hypokalemia. Monitor electrolytes regularly.

URGENCY LEGEND

Emergency / Immediate Primary / First-line Secondary / Alternative Monitoring / Supportive

🧪 Cortisol Transport — "75-15-10 Rule"
75 · 15 · 10

The binding proportions that explain cortisol's long half-life (60-90 min)

75%
Transcortin (CBG) — tightly bound to cortisol-binding globulin. Largest fraction.
15%
Albumin — loosely bound to albumin. Available reservoir.
10%
Free / Active — ONLY this fraction is biologically active! Because so little is free, degradation is slow → long half-life.
"10% is circulating unbound... this makes its half-life long." — Exam pearl
🩺 Conn's vs Addison's Electrolytes — "FLIP"
CONN FLIPS ADDISON

Conn's and Addison's have exact opposite electrolyte patterns — just flip them!

Conn's Syndrome

Na⁺ ↑ Hypernatremia
K⁺ ↓ Hypokalemia
BP ↑ Hypertension
pH ↑ Alkalosis

Addison's Disease

Na⁺ ↓ Hyponatremia
K⁺ ↑ Hyperkalemia
BP ↓ Hypotension
pH ↓ Acidosis

💡 Think of them as mirror images!
