Topic Overview
All high-yield points at a glance
⚠️
EXAM FAVORITE: Areola develops at 5th month. Accessory nipple = Polythelia. Skin pitting = Cooper's ligaments. 60% carcinomas in upper lateral quadrant.
⭐
CLINICAL PEARL: Left subclavian trunk → thoracic duct. Right → right lymphatic duct. Always read L vs R carefully!
🔑 Key Concept Cards
Embryology
Mammary Gland Origin
Ectodermal thickening (milk ridge) → 15–20 solid cords → canalization → lactiferous ducts → nipple elevation. It is a modified sweat gland.
⚠️ Exam
Congenital
Polythelia vs Polymastia
Polythelia: Extra nipple — persistence of mammary line fragments. Polymastia: Complete accessory breast from full mammary remnant development.
⭐ High Yield
Structure
Breast Boundaries
Vertical: 2nd → 6th rib. Horizontal: Lateral sternal margin → midaxillary line. Contains 15–20 lobes, each with a lactiferous duct.
⚠️ Exam
Clinical
Cooper's Ligaments
Fibrous septa (suspensory ligaments) separating lobes. Carcinoma tension → skin pitting. Retromammary space invasion → chest wall fixation.
💎 Pearl⚠️ Exam
Blood Supply
Arterial Sources
Medial: Internal thoracic artery. Lateral: Axillary branches (superior thoracic, thoracoacromial, lateral thoracic, subscapular). Deep: Intercostal perforators (2–4).
⭐ High Yield
Lymphatics
Drainage Quadrants
Lateral (75%) → Anterior axillary (pectoral). Medial (25%) → Internal thoracic (parasternal). 60% of carcinomas in upper lateral quadrant.
⚠️ Exam💎 Pearl
🕐 Developmental Timeline
Early Fetal Life — Milk Ridge
Linear ectodermal thickening from axilla to groin. In humans, all regresses except the small pectoral portion.
Solid Cord Formation
Pectoral ectodermal thickening sends 15–20 solid cords growing into the underlying mesenchyme.
5th Month — Areola Recognized ⚠️
Areola becomes visible as a circular pigmented area around the future nipple. High-yield exam fact.
Canalization of Cords
Solid cords canalize → lactiferous ducts. Mesenchyme proliferates, elevating the depressed ectoderm to form the nipple.
Puberty — Ovarian Hormones
Ducts elongate; increased breast size mainly from fat deposition (not lobe multiplication) under ovarian hormone influence.
Adult — Structural Maturity
15–20 lobes in superficial fascia, each with lactiferous duct with dilated ampulla opening at nipple apex.
🩻 Clinical Signs Grid
🔵
Skin Pitting / Dimpling
Carcinoma creates tension on Cooper's (suspensory) ligaments, pulling the overlying skin inward.
⚠️ Exam
🔴
Nipple Retraction (Acquired)
Tumor pulls on lactiferous ducts, drawing the nipple inward. Distinct from congenital retracted nipple.
⭐ High Yield
🟤
Breast Fixation
Carcinoma invasion of the retromammary space destroys loose CT, fixing the breast to pectoralis major fascia.
💎 Pearl
🟢
Axillary Lymphadenopathy
60% of carcinomas arise in upper lateral quadrant → pectoral nodes first → central → apical chain.
⚠️ Exam
🟡
Peau d'Orange
Superficial lymphatic blockage causes skin oedema; tethered hair follicles create an orange-peel appearance.
💎 Pearl
🔵
Contralateral / Abdominal Spread
Obstruction of normal lymphatics forces retrograde flow to opposite breast or abdominal cavity.

🌊 Lymphatics & Pathology
Axillary node groups, drainage pathways, and carcinoma spread
⚠️
60% of breast carcinomas arise in the upper lateral quadrant → spread first to anterior (pectoral) axillary nodes.
🫧 6 Axillary Lymph Node Groups
Group 1
Anterior (Pectoral)
Behind lower border of pectoralis major. Drains lateral quadrants (75%) + anterolateral abdominal wall above umbilicus.
⚠️ 1st in carcinoma
Group 2
Posterior (Subscapular)
In front of subscapularis. Receives superficial lymph from the back down to iliac crests.
Group 3
Lateral
Medial side of axillary vein. Drains entire upper limb except those draining with the cephalic vein (infraclavicular).
Group 4
Central
In center of axilla in axillary fat. Relay station — receives efferents from anterior, posterior, and lateral groups.
Group 5
Infraclavicular (Deltopectoral)
Outside the axilla — in groove between deltoid and pectoralis major. Drains medial hand/forearm via cephalic vein route.
⭐ Outside axilla
Group 6
Apical
Apex of axilla at 1st rib. Receives efferents from ALL other axillary nodes → subclavian lymph trunk → thoracic duct (L) or right lymph trunk (R).
⚠️ Terminal relay

↔️ Alternate Spread Pathways
⚠️
Contralateral Breast Spread
Obstruction of normal lymphatic pathways forces lymph through communicating vessels to the opposite breast.
⚠️
Abdominal Cavity Spread
Retrograde flow through obstructed lymphatics allows cells to reach the abdominal cavity via subdiaphragmatic connections.
🦴
Bone Metastasis
Via haematogenous spread — cancer cells enter blood vessels. Accounts for distant bone metastases when local lymphatics are overwhelmed.
🫀
Parasternal Spread (Medial)
Medial quadrant tumors (25%) drain to internal thoracic (parasternal) nodes → broncho-mediastinal trunks.
