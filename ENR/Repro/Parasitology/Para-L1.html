🦠 Toxoplasma gondii — The Essentials

An obligate intracellular parasite infecting up to 1/3 of the global human population. Cats are the definitive hosts (sexual cycle); humans and other warm-blooded mammals serve as intermediate hosts (asexual cycle). Largely asymptomatic in immunocompetent adults, but can be life-threatening in immunocompromised patients and devastating in congenital infection.

Felids (Cats)

Sexual cycle occurs in the cat's intestine. Millions of oocysts shed daily in feces. Humans/animals = intermediate hosts (asexual cycle only).

☣️

Multiple Pathways

Raw/undercooked meat (tissue cysts), contaminated water/food (oocysts), transplacental, blood transfusion, organ transplant, lab accidents.

Chorioretinitis + Hydrocephalus + Cerebral Calcification. Worst outcomes in 1st trimester. ⚠️ Exam favourite.

🧠
Immunocompromised
AIDS / Transplant

3rd leading cause of death in AIDS patients. Reactivation of bradyzoites → meningoencephalitis. Classic MRI: ring-enhancing brain lesions.

💊
Key Drug Fact
Atovaquone = Cysts

Only Atovaquone targets encysted bradyzoites. All other drugs (Pyrimethamine, Sulfadiazine) only kill tachyzoites. ⚠️ Exam favourite.

🥚
Oocyst Maturation
1–2 Weeks in Soil

Freshly shed oocysts are non-infectious. They require 1–2 weeks of environmental sporulation to become infective (disporocystic tetrazoic).

Life Cycle Timeline

🐈 Sexual Cycle — Definitive Host (Cat)

Cat ingests tissue cysts. Sexual reproduction in the intestine. Millions of unsporulated, non-infectious oocysts shed daily in feces.

🌱 Environmental Sporulation (1–2 weeks in soil)

Oocysts mature into the infective disporocystic tetrazoic form — 2 sporocysts, each with 4 sporozoites. Now highly infectious.

🧑 Intermediate Host Ingestion (Human)

Human ingests sporulated oocysts (contaminated food/water) or tissue cysts (raw meat). Parasites released in the intestine.

⚡ Acute Phase — Tachyzoites (Pseudocysts)

Parasites transform into rapidly multiplying tachyzoites (6×3 µm, crescent-shaped). They invade nucleated cells (especially macrophages), forming pseudocysts (no true wall). Widespread dissemination.

🛡️ Immune Response Mounted

The host immune system attacks tachyzoites. The parasite evades by transforming into slow-replicating bradyzoites and migrating to immune-privileged sites.

😴 Chronic Phase — Bradyzoites (True Cysts)

Bradyzoites enclosed in true cyst wall (50–100 µm) reside lifelong in CNS, muscle, and eyes. Dormant unless immunosuppression occurs. Targeted ONLY by Atovaquone.

🔴 Reactivation (Immunocompromised)

If immunity falls (AIDS, transplant), cysts rupture. Bradyzoites revert to tachyzoites → severe meningoencephalitis, ring-enhancing brain lesions.

Key Clinical Signs & Pearls

🟢 Asymptomatic (Immunocompetent)

>80% have no symptoms. Mild flu-like illness or cervical lymphadenopathy in 10–20%.

High-Yield

💡 Cervical Lymphadenopathy

Most common symptomatic presentation in immunocompetent acquired toxoplasmosis.

Clinical Pearl

🧠 Ring-Enhancing Brain Lesions

Classic MRI finding in reactivated CNS toxoplasmosis in AIDS patients. Multiple, bilateral.

⚠️ High-Yield

👁️ Chorioretinitis

Eye inflammation. Part of congenital triad. May also occur with reactivation. Treat with corticosteroids.

Exam Favourite

💧 Hydrocephalus (Congenital)

CSF flow obstruction due to cerebral damage by tachyzoites crossing the placenta in 1st trimester.

⚠️ Congenital Triad

🪨 Cerebral Calcifications

Intracranial calcifications visible on CT/X-ray in congenital toxoplasmosis. Part of the classic triad.

⚠️ Congenital Triad

🧬 Neuropsychiatric Disorders

Latent infection linked to neuropsychiatric manifestations. Heavily affects the CNS in immunocompromised patients.

Clinical Pearl

🤱 Congenital — 1st Trimester = Worst

Early infection → abortion, stillbirth, or severe triad. Late pregnancy: more frequent but less severe defects.

Exam Favourite

💀 3rd Cause of Death in AIDS

Reactivated toxoplasmosis is the 3rd leading cause of AIDS-related mortality. Prophylaxis with TMP-SMX when CD4 <100.

💊 Treatment

Regimens by urgency and patient group. Drug comparison table included.

🚨 Active / Acute Infection

First-Line Regimen

Pyrimethamine
Antifolate — inhibits DHFR. Kills tachyzoites. The backbone of standard therapy. Tachyzoites only
Sulfadiazine
Sulfonamide — synergistic with Pyrimethamine. Inhibits folate synthesis. Kills tachyzoites. Tachyzoites only
Folinic Acid (Leucovorin)
Added to prevent bone marrow suppression from Pyrimethamine. Does NOT reduce efficacy against parasite.
⚠️ Key Combination: Pyrimethamine + Sulfadiazine + Folinic Acid
🤰 Pregnancy

Prevent Trans-Placental Transmission

Spiramycin
Macrolide antibiotic. Concentrates in placenta. Reduces risk of transplacental transmission to the fetus. Duration: 4 weeks. Cannot be replaced by Pyrimethamine (teratogenic in 1st trimester). ⚠️ Exam Favourite
😴 Chronic / Encysted Forms

Targeting Bradyzoites

Atovaquone
The ONLY drug effective against encysted bradyzoites (true cysts). All other drugs are inactive against the cyst stage. Mechanism: inhibits mitochondrial electron transport. ⚠️ Exam Favourite
👁️ Adjunctive Therapy

Managing Inflammation

Systemic Corticosteroids
Added to anti-parasitic regimen to alleviate severe inflammation, specifically chorioretinitis. Do NOT kill parasites. Do NOT use alone.
👶 Neonates — Congenital Toxoplasmosis

Extended Treatment

Pyrimethamine + Sulfadiazine + Folinic Acid
Standard combination, but for one full year in neonates. Spiramycin is for the mother, NOT the neonate. ⚠️ Exam distinction.
