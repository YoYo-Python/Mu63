🌡️ Topic 1 — Inflammatory & Benign Disease
🦠 Breast Abscess Inflammatory
Painful collection of pus, usually caused by Staphylococcus aureus entering via nipple abrasion or retained milk. Most common inflammatory condition of the breast.
🔧 Mechanism: S. aureus → nipple abrasion/lactation → bacterial invasion of breast parenchyma → pyogenic inflammation → abscess formation
📐 Morphology: Acute/chronic; single/multiple; exquisitely tender mass. Organisms: primarily S. aureus.
⚠️ Clinical: Predominantly affects nursing mothers (puerperal mastitis). Predisposed by poor nipple hygiene.
🧫 Mammary Duct Ectasia Benign
Dilatation of mammary ducts due to thickening (inspissation) of breast secretions. Triggers periductal plasma cell infiltrate. Mimics carcinoma clinically.
🔧 Mechanism: Inspissation of secretions → duct dilation → rupture of duct wall → periductal inflammation → plasma cell infiltrate (hence "periductal plasma cell mastitis")
📐 Morphology: Dilated ducts filled with granular secretion; surrounding chronic inflammation dominated by plasma cells.
⚠️ Clinical: Firm subareolar mass + nipple discharge ± retraction. Danger: clinically mimics cancer!
🫧 Fibrocystic Disease Most Common
Most common breast disease — affects >50% of reproductive-age women. Caused by hyperestrogenemia disrupting normal cyclic changes. Characterized by fibrosis, adenosis, epitheliosis, and cyst formation.
🔧 Mechanism: Hyperestrogenemia → abnormal cyclic breast response → 4 components: (1) Fibrosis, (2) Adenosis (↑ acini/lobules), (3) Epitheliosis (↑ epithelial cells in ducts), (4) Cyst formation
📐 Morphology: Bilateral, ill-defined, rubbery, mobile masses. Cysts may contain serous/hemorrhagic fluid. Blue-dome cysts.
⚠️ Malignant potential: Generally NOT premalignant. EXCEPT: Atypical epitheliosis/hyperplasia → increases breast cancer risk (5× if atypical + family history)
🎀 Fibroadenoma Commonest Benign Tumor
Commonest benign tumor of the breast. Mixed fibrous + epithelial proliferation driven by estrogen stimulation. Peaks in the 3rd decade. Classic: firm, freely mobile, encapsulated "breast mouse."
🔧 Mechanism: Estrogen stimulation → mixed fibroepithelial proliferation in TDLU → encapsulated mass
📐 Subtypes: (1) Pericanalicular — stroma encircles open round ducts. (2) Intracanalicular — stroma compresses ducts into slits. (3) Mixed.
⚠️ Clinical: Single, firm, painless, well-defined, freely mobile. Does NOT typically undergo malignant transformation. Regresses post-menopause.
🌿 Phyllodes Tumor Unpredictable
Large fibroepithelial tumor with pronounced, hypercellular stromal overgrowth forming leaf-like (phyllodes) architecture. Can be benign, borderline, or malignant. ~10% metastasize via stromal component.
🔧 Mechanism: Hypercellular stromal proliferation (not epithelial) → leaf-like clefts → mass effect
📐 Morphology: Huge rounded mass. Leaf-like fronds on cut section. Stromal cells are the malignant component (not epithelial).
⚠️ Clinical: Older females (vs. fibroadenoma in younger). Metastases if malignant are stromal only. Treatment: wide excision.
💧 Duct Papilloma Intraductal
Intraductal growth within the main lactiferous ducts beneath the nipple. Most common cause of bloody/serous nipple discharge. 4th–5th decade. Small risk of papillary carcinoma transformation.
🔧 Mechanism: Epithelial proliferation → papillary projections with fibrovascular core within lactiferous ducts → hemorrhage into duct lumen
📐 Morphology: Small pedunculated mass with fibrovascular stalks; epithelium ± apocrine metaplasia.
⚠️ Clinical: Sub-areolar mass + bloody nipple discharge. Rare malignant transformation (→ papillary carcinoma).
🎗️ Topic 2 — Breast Carcinoma
🔴 Invasive Duct Carcinoma (NOS) 65–80% of cases
Commonest breast cancer type. Tumor cells invade stroma, inciting a desmoplastic (fibrous) reaction → stony hard mass. Causes skin tethering and nipple retraction.
🔧 Mechanism: Malignant ductal cells → stromal invasion → desmoplastic (fibrous) stromal reaction → fixation to skin/pectoral muscle
📐 Morphology: Ill-defined, hard, gritty cut surface. Irregular stellate border. Dense fibrous stroma.
⚠️ Clinical: Hard, fixed, painless mass. Skin dimpling/tethering. Nipple retraction. ±Peau d'orange.
🏹 Invasive Lobular Carcinoma Indian File Pattern
Carcinoma arising from lobular units. Dyscohesive small uniform cells infiltrate in single-file strands. Tends to be multicentric and bilateral. Worse prognosis than NOS.
🔧 Mechanism: Loss of E-cadherin → cell dyscohesion → individual cells infiltrate stroma in single rows ("Indian file")
📐 Morphology: Small, uniform cells in single rows. Targetoid/concentric arrangement around ducts. Little pleomorphism.
⚠️ Clinical: Multicentric + bilateral = screen other breast! Worse prognosis. Metastasizes to unusual sites (GI, uterus, meninges).
🟣 Medullary Carcinoma BRCA1-associated
Well-circumscribed fleshy mass. Microscopically: solid sheets of large cells + heavy lymphocytic/plasmacytic infiltrate + scant stroma. Paradoxically better prognosis than NOS.
🔧 Mechanism: Associated with BRCA1 mutations. Dense immune infiltrate (good host response) → better prognosis
📐 Morphology: Fleshy, soft, well-circumscribed mass. Microscopically: syncytial sheets of large cells, brisk lymphocytic infiltrate.
⚠️ Clinical: May mimic benign lesion on imaging. Despite high-grade histology, prognosis better than NOS.
🫧 Mucinous (Colloid) Carcinoma Best Prognosis
Characterized by large lakes of extracellular mucin containing floating islands of malignant cells. Soft, gelatinous appearance. Good prognosis despite being malignant.
🔧 Mechanism: Tumor cells produce massive extracellular mucin → cells float in "lakes" of mucin → slow local growth
📐 Morphology: Gelatinous cut surface. Mucin lakes with small islands/clusters of malignant cells. Well-circumscribed.
⚠️ Clinical: Older women. Slow growing. Best prognosis among invasive carcinomas.
🔵 Paget's Disease of Nipple Always has underlying cancer
Eczematous lesion of nipple/areola. Duct carcinoma extends along main mammary duct → infiltrates nipple epidermis. Large pale vacuolated Paget's cells seen in thickened epidermis.
🔧 Mechanism: Ductal carcinoma cells migrate via lactiferous ducts → infiltrate nipple epidermis → Paget's cells (large, pale, mucin-rich) appear in epidermis
📐 Morphology: Paget's cells: large, pale, vacuolated with prominent nucleoli. Located within stratified squamous epithelium.
⚠️ Clinical: Always indicates underlying ductal carcinoma! Differential: eczema (bilateral vs. Paget's which is unilateral).
🍊 Peau d'Orange Advanced sign
Orange-peel appearance of breast skin. Tumor cells block dermal lymphatics → lymphedema of skin. Hair follicles remain tethered → dimpled appearance resembling orange skin.
🔧 Mechanism: Tumor permeation of dermal lymphatics → lymphatic obstruction → skin edema. Hair follicles are tethered to underlying dermis by fibrous bands → cannot lift with the edematous skin → dimples form at each follicle
📐 Morphology: Grossly thickened, dimpled, orange-peel skin over tumor. Microscopically: dilated dermal lymphatics packed with tumor emboli.
⚠️ Clinical: Sign of advanced lymphatic involvement. Often associated with inflammatory breast carcinoma.
⚔️ Cancer en Cuirasse Very Advanced
Extensive skin involvement where tumor nodules coalesce to form a rigid plate-like encasement of the chest wall, resembling a breastplate (cuirasse). Very late-stage manifestation.
🔧 Mechanism: Extensive tumor cell permeation of dermal/subdermal lymphatics + direct skin infiltration → coalescent skin nodules → rigid fibrous plate
📐 Morphology: Hard, thickened, fixed skin with nodular masses and ulceration.
⚠️ Clinical: Very poor prognosis. Indicates end-stage local disease.
