Research Methodology

Study Guide · Descriptive Studies & Research Fundamentals
Core Concepts
10 key points to master
  • Research is the systematic collection, description, analysis, and interpretation of data to answer a question or solve a problem.
  • A strong research question is evaluated using the FINER criteria: Feasible, Interesting, Novel, Ethical, and Relevant.
  • A study's "Aim" is a broad statement of overall intention, while an "Objective" details exactly how to achieve the aim and must be SMART (Specific, Measurable, Applicable, Relevant, Time-bound).
  • Descriptive studies are observational designs that lack a comparison group and an assigned intervention.
  • Descriptive studies are utilized strictly to formulate or generate research hypotheses, not to test them.
  • Case Reports document a single unusual clinical case.
  • Case Series detail multiple similar cases that can signal the start of an epidemic.
  • Ecological (correlational) studies search for exposure-outcome associations using existing population-level data (e.g., vital statistics) rather than individual data.
  • Cross-sectional (prevalence) studies provide a single snapshot in time to estimate disease prevalence and exposure proportions.
  • Longitudinal (incidence) studies perform repeated follow-up observations on a community to measure disease incidence and natural history.
  • Survey studies are descriptive field studies conducted to investigate community problems, perform case finding, and determine problem magnitude.

Exam Traps
8 common pitfalls to avoid
High-Yield Exam Traps
  • Do not confuse Aim with Objective; an Aim is a broad goal, while an Objective must be highly specific and SMART.
  • Cross-sectional studies are strictly for calculating disease prevalence, never incidence.
  • Cross-sectional studies are useless for highly fatal, short-duration, or acute diseases.
  • Descriptive studies only formulate hypotheses; they cannot test hypotheses or prove absolute causation.
  • Ecological studies strictly use aggregate population-level data, never individual patient data.
  • A case report describes one unique patient, whereas a case series describes a group of similar cases and can indicate a new epidemic.
  • The very first step of the research process is formulating the research problem, not picking the study design.
  • Longitudinal studies frequently suffer from loss to follow-up, a complication that does not apply to cross-sectional studies.
