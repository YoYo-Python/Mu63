Cohort Study — Complete Guide

Core Concepts Essential definitions & principles

  • Cohort Study: An observational, analytic, prospective (longitudinal) study comparing disease incidence over time.
  • Cohort: A group of individuals sharing a common characteristic (e.g., all smokers).
  • Exposed Group: Individuals possessing the specific risk factor under investigation.
  • Non-Exposed (Control) Group: Individuals lacking the specific risk factor.
  • Initial Step: All participants must be entirely free of the investigated disease at the study's onset.
  • Indication (Rare Exposure): Highly efficient for studying rare exposures (e.g., industrial chemicals).
  • Indication (Multiple Outcomes): Ideal for evaluating multiple disease outcomes arising from a single exposure.
  • Key Output: Calculates precise Incidence Rates, Relative Risk (RR), and Attributable Risk (AR).

Formulas & Calculations Key equations with worked examples

Key Formulas
Overall Incidence (a + c) / (a + b + c + d)
Incidence Exposed (Ie) a / (a + b)
Incidence Non-Exposed (In) c / (c + d)
Relative Risk (RR) Ie / In
Attributable Risk (AR) Ie − In
  Scenario 1: Smoking & CHD
100 smokers and 100 non-smokers are followed. 60 smokers develop CHD. 20 non-smokers develop CHD. Calculate RR and AR.
1
Ie (Incidence in Exposed) = 60 / 100 = 0.6
2
In (Incidence in Non-Exposed) = 20 / 100 = 0.2
3
RR = Ie / In = 0.6 / 0.2 = 3
4
AR = Ie − In = 0.6 − 0.2 = 0.4
RR = 3 (Smokers have 3× the risk)  |  AR = 40/100 attributed to smoking
  Scenario 2: OCP & Breast Cancer
100 OCP users and 100 non-users followed for 10 years. 20 OCP users get breast cancer. 15 non-users get breast cancer. Calculate Overall Incidence and RR.
1
Overall Incidence = (20 + 15) / (100 + 100) = 35 / 200
2
Ie = 20 / 100
3
In = 15 / 100
4
RR = Ie / In = (20/100) / (15/100) = 4/3 ≈ 1.33
Overall Incidence = 35/200  |  RR = 4/3
  Scenario 3: Diet & Obesity
50 individuals eat a specific high-calorie food, 50 do not. 20 exposed become obese, 10 unexposed become obese. Calculate AR.
1
Ie = 20 / 50 = 0.4
2
In = 10 / 50 = 0.2
3
AR = Ie − In = 0.4 − 0.2 = 0.2
AR = 10/50 = 20% of obesity cases attributed to the dietary exposure

Exam Traps High-yield pitfalls to avoid

Common Exam Pitfalls — Don't Fall For These!
  • The "Zero" Trap Students often mistakenly think no association means RR = 0. The correct value for no association is RR = 1.
  • The Baseline Trap You must exclude anyone who already has the disease at the start of a cohort study. Participants must be completely disease-free.
  • The "Rare" Reversal Case-control is for rare diseases; Cohort is for rare exposures. Do not swap these.
  • The Multiple vs. Single Trap Cohort studies track one exposure to find multiple outcomes (like branches on a tree). Case-control studies track one outcome backward to find multiple exposures.
  • The Bias Trap The most critical bias in Cohort studies is "Loss to follow-up" (dropouts), while Case-control suffers from "Recall bias".
  • The "Cohort" Definition Trick The word "cohort" on an exam doesn't just mean the study type; it strictly translates to "a group with a common characteristic" (like military troops).
