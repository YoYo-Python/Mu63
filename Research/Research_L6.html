Core Concepts

Key definitions and foundational ideas in Medical Statistics

  • Medical Statistics involves collecting, presenting, analyzing, and drawing inferential conclusions from medical data.

  • Its main uses include simplifying massive data, facilitating comparison, testing hypotheses, and guiding health policies.

  • Target population is the total group of interest, while the sampling frame is the actual list from which the sample is drawn.

  • A sample is a representative subset used because studying entire populations is costly, time-consuming, and often impossible.

  • Data represents raw, discrete observations, whereas information is processed, meaningful data providing relevant context.

  • Variables are measurable traits varying between individuals, divided broadly into qualitative (categorical) and quantitative (numerical) groups.

  • Random probability sampling ensures every individual unit has an equal and independent chance of selection.

  • Non-random probability sampling does not involve random selection and is used for quick access or hard-to-reach targets.

  • Measurement scales progress in complexity from basic categorical labels (nominal) to precise measurements with absolute true zeros (ratio).

Math Formulas

Key formulas with clinical scenario walkthroughs

Sampling Interval Formula
k = N / n
Where k = Sampling Interval, N = Total Population, n = Desired Sample Size
1 A factory employs 2700 workers and you need a systematic random sample of 300 workers.
Show Steps
Divide the total population (2700) by the sample size (300).
k = 2700 / 300 = 9
Sampling interval = 9 → Select every 9th worker
2 You have a list of 1000 first-year medical students and want a systematic random sample of 100 students.
Show Steps
Divide the population (1000) by the sample size (100).
k = 1000 / 100 = 10
Sampling interval = 10 → Select every 10th student
3 You are studying 2700 factory workers and decide to use a systematic interval of every 9th worker.
Show Steps
Divide the total population (2700) by the sampling interval (9).
n = 2700 / 9 = 300
Desired sample size = 300 workers

Exam Traps

Common pitfalls and frequently confused concepts

Confusing Interval and Ratio Scales
Interval scales have an arbitrary zero (e.g., Celsius temperature), whereas Ratio scales have a true absolute zero meaning absence of the trait (e.g., weight).
Stratified vs. Cluster Sampling
Stratified sampling divides a heterogeneous population into distinct subgroups, whereas Cluster sampling selects pre-existing intact homogeneous groups.
Nominal vs. Ordinal Variables
Ordinal categorical variables require a strict logical ranking (e.g., pain scale); Nominal variables do not have any inherent order (e.g., blood group).
Simple Random vs. Systematic Sampling
Both require homogeneous populations, but systematic sampling strictly uses a predetermined mathematical interval.
Parameter vs. Statistic Terminology
A Parameter always describes the entire population, while a Statistic only describes a numerical value summarizing sample data.
Discrete vs. Continuous Numericals
Discrete numerical variables never allow fractions (e.g., number of pregnancies); continuous variables permit infinite fractions (e.g., hemoglobin level).
Quota vs. Stratified Sampling
Quota sampling is explicitly a non-random, non-probability method, whereas Stratified sampling is a random probability method.
Snowball Sampling Indication
This non-random method is tested as the exclusive choice for hidden, stigmatized, or inaccessible populations like drug addicts.
