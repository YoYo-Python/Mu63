Core Concepts

Foundational definitions and classifications in medical statistics.

Medical Statistics
  • Science of collecting, summarizing (descriptive), and predicting/testing hypotheses (inferential) from clinical data.
Qualitative Data
  • Nominal: Categorical variables with no inherent order (e.g., blood group).
  • Ordinal: Categorical variables with a logical ranking (e.g., disease stage).
Quantitative Data
  • Discrete: Whole numbers only (e.g., number of patients).
  • Continuous: Decimals allowed (e.g., serum calcium, height).
Central Tendency
  • Identifies the center of a data distribution using Mean, Median, or Mode.
Dispersion
  • Describes the spread of data using Range, Variance, and Standard Deviation (SD).
Mean Indications
  • Exclusively used for summarizing normally distributed, continuous data lacking extreme outliers.
Median Indications
  • Preferred for skewed, non-parametric continuous data with extreme outliers, or qualitative ordinal data.
Histogram Indications
  • Graphing method strictly used for continuous quantitative variables, represented by fused bars without gaps.
Scatter Diagram Indications
  • Used exclusively to illustrate the correlation (positive or negative) between two quantitative continuous variables.

Formulas & Scenarios

Key formulas and step-by-step clinical worked examples.

Core Formulas
  • Mean: x̄ = Σx / n
  • Median Rank (Odd n): (n + 1) / 2
  • Median Rank (Even n): Avg of (n/2) & (n/2)+1 positions
  • Variance (S²): Σ(x − x̄)² / (n − 1)
  • Standard Deviation: SD = √Variance
Scenario 1: Calculating Mean Show Steps

Scenario: Pediatric weights (kg): 34, 41, 37, 32, 36.

  • 1 Sum all values: Σx = 34 + 41 + 37 + 32 + 36 = 180
  • 2 Divide by total count: n = 5
Solution: 180 / 5 = 36 kg
Scenario 2: Calculating Median (Odd n) Show Steps

Scenario: Patient ages: 14, 5, 9, 24, 4, 16, 10.

  • 1 Sort ascending: 4, 5, 9, 10, 14, 16, 24
  • 2 Find rank: (7 + 1) / 2 = 4th position
Solution: The 4th value is 10
Scenario 3: Calculating Median (Even n) Show Steps

Scenario: Data values: 3, 4, 6, 10, 13, 19.

  • 1 Already sorted: 3, 4, 6, 10, 13, 19
  • 2 Middle values are 3rd (6) and 4th (10)
Solution: (6 + 10) / 2 = 8
Scenario 4: Calculating Standard Deviation Show Steps

Scenario: Glucose levels: 6, 8, 10, 10, 14 (Mean = 10).

  • 1 Find deviations from mean: −4, −2, 0, 0, +4
  • 2 Square each deviation: 16, 4, 0, 0, 16
  • 3 Sum of squares = 36
  • 4 Variance = 36 / (5 − 1) = 9
Solution: SD = √9 = ±3

Exam Traps

Common pitfalls designed to trick you. Study these carefully.

High-Yield Exam Traps
1
Assuming clinical stages (Stage I, II, III) are continuous numbers; they are strictly qualitative ordinal data.
2
Calculating the mean for skewed data with extreme outliers instead of the median.
3
Choosing a histogram to present nominal or discrete data — histograms are ONLY for continuous data.
4
Forgetting to arrange raw data in ascending order before locating the median.
5
Confusing the mathematical rank/order of the median (e.g., "the 4th value") with the actual median value itself.
6
Categorizing "number of patients/children" as continuous; it is always quantitative discrete because humans cannot be fractioned.
7
Confusing Variance with Standard Deviation; remember SD is always the square root of Variance.
8
Missing that analytical tables always display a P-value to test significance, whereas descriptive tables only show frequencies and percentages.
