Core Concepts

Foundational definitions and principles

Data Distribution Types

Medical statistics primarily categorizes data distributions into normal (symmetrical) and non-normal (skewed) distributions.

Normal Distribution

A symmetrical, bell-shaped probability curve where the Mean = Median = Mode and all are located at the exact midpoint of the base.

Skewed Distribution

An asymmetrical curve indicating non-normal data:

  • Positive Skew: Tail extends to the right — Mean > Median > Mode
  • Negative Skew: Tail extends to the left — Mean < Median < Mode

Null Hypothesis (H₀)

A fundamental statistical assumption proposing that there is no difference or no association between the tested groups.

Alternative Hypothesis (H₁)

The counterpart to the null hypothesis, proposing that a measurable difference or association truly exists between the groups.

P-value Interpretation

A calculation representing the probability that an observed difference happened by chance. A threshold of p < 0.05 is used to reject H₀ and accept H₁.

Student t-test (Independent)

Indicated specifically for comparing the means of two distinct groups when the variable is quantitative and normally distributed.

Chi-square Test (χ²)

Indicated for evaluating the association or comparing frequencies of qualitative (categorical) variables among two or more groups.

  • Total observations must be > 20
  • Expected cell frequencies must be > 5

Fisher Exact Test

Indicated as an alternative for qualitative categorical data when the sample is small — specifically when ≥ 2 expected cell frequencies (or 50% of cells) are < 5.

Formulas & Calculations

Key formulas with worked examples

Normal Distribution SD Ranges
Mean ± 1 SD = 68.2% of the data Mean ± 2 SD = 95.45% of the data Mean ± 3 SD = 99.73% of the data
Scenario 1: Calculating 1 SD Range
Clinical Scenario: You are observing 1,000 men with a mean systolic blood pressure (SBP) of 120 mmHg and an SD of 10 mmHg. How many men have a pressure between 110 and 130 mmHg?
Show Steps
Identify the range: 110 = (120 − 10) and 130 = (120 + 10), which corresponds to Mean ± 1 SD.
Recall the rule: Mean ± 1 SD encompasses roughly 68% of the data.
Calculate: 0.68 × 1,000 = 680 men.
✓ Answer: 680 men
Scenario 2: Calculating 2 SD Intervals
Clinical Scenario: Serum cholesterol in a population is normally distributed with a mean of 170 mg/dl and an SD of 8 mg/dl. What interval encompasses approximately 95% of the values?
Show Steps
Identify the percentage rule: 95% of data corresponds to Mean ± 2 SD.
Multiply SD by 2: 2 × 8 = 16.
Apply to mean: (170 − 16) to (170 + 16) = 154 to 186 mg/dl.
✓ Answer: 154 – 186 mg/dl
Scenario 3: Total Affected Population
Clinical Scenario: Out of 1,000 men with a mean SBP of 120 mmHg and an SD of 10 mmHg, how many are expected to have an SBP ranging from 100 to 140 mmHg?
Show Steps
Identify the range: 100 = (120 − 20) and 140 = (120 + 20), representing exactly Mean ± 2 SD.
Recall the rule: Mean ± 2 SD encompasses 95% of the population.
Calculate: 0.95 × 1,000 = 950 men.
✓ Answer: 950 men

Exam Traps

Common pitfalls and how to avoid them

Trap #1

Confusing Test Indications: Never apply the Student t-test to categorical (qualitative) outcome frequencies like "improved vs. not improved"; always use the Chi-square test for categorical variables.

Trap #2

Misinterpreting Borderline P-values: A p-value of exactly 0.06 is greater than the standard 0.05 threshold; you must strictly accept the null hypothesis and declare the result statistically non-significant.

Trap #3

Misidentifying Skew Direction: The term "positive skew" describes the direction of the elongated tail pointing toward positive values on the right, not where the main bulk/peak of the data resides.

Trap #4

Mixing Mean/Median/Mode Orders: Remember that extreme outliers pull the mean the hardest; thus, in a positively skewed dataset, the Mean is always the highest value (Mean > Median > Mode).

Trap #5

Ignoring Expected Cell Count Limits: Blindly applying the Chi-square test to small tables is a common trap; if any expected cell frequency falls below 5, you must pivot to the Fisher Exact test.

Trap #6

SD Percentage Confusion: Mixing up standard deviation empirical rules is common; memorize exactly 68.2% for 1 SD, 95.5% for 2 SD, and 99.7% for 3 SD.

Trap #7

Raw Number vs. Percentage Trap: Skimming questions too fast and selecting a percentage (e.g., "95%") when the exam explicitly asks for the absolute number of affected individuals (e.g., "950 out of 1000").

Trap #8

Misunderstanding the Null Hypothesis: Students often mistake the researcher's clinical goal (the Alternative hypothesis) for the Null; the Null hypothesis always conservatively asserts "no difference" or "no association".

Trap #9

Failing to Spot Non-Normal Data via SD: If a clinical scenario provides a Standard Deviation value that is significantly larger than the Mean, this is a hidden clue that the data is highly dispersed and non-normally distributed (skewed).
