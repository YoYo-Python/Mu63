Core Concepts

Key definitions and fundamentals of morbidity measurement

Definitions
  • Morbidity statistics enumerate the extent, frequency, and severity of diseases in a community.
  • Incidence rate measures the occurrence of new cases in an at-risk population over a defined period.
  • Prevalence rate measures existing cases (old and new) in an examined population at a given time.
  • Point prevalence captures existing cases at a single time point (a snapshot).
  • Period prevalence captures existing plus new cases over a specific duration of time.
Indications & Uses
  • Used for community diagnosis and identifying the overall disease burden.
  • Crucial for health planning and optimal medical resource allocation.
  • Evaluates intervention programs and the efficacy of health services.

Formulas & Calculations

Key equations with worked examples and step-by-step solutions

Key Formulas
Incidence Rate = (New cases ÷ At-risk population) × Constant
Prevalence Rate = (Old + New cases ÷ Examined population) × Constant
Scenario 1

A city has 500,000 residents. A screening detects 5,000 TB cases. Find the point prevalence.

Show Steps
Formula: Point Prevalence = (Total existing cases ÷ Total population) × Constant

Step 1: Numerator = 5,000 (detected TB cases)
Step 2: Denominator = 500,000 (total population)
Step 3: Calculate: (5,000 ÷ 500,000) × 1,000
✅ Answer: 10 per 1,000
Scenario 2

One year later, 50 new TB cases are found in the same city. Find the incidence rate.

Show Steps
Formula: Incidence Rate = (New cases ÷ At-risk population) × Constant

Step 1: Numerator = 50 (new cases only)
Step 2: Denominator = 500,000 − 5,000 = 495,000 (exclude existing cases!)
Step 3: Calculate: (50 ÷ 495,000) × 100,000
✅ Answer: 10.1 per 100,000
Scenario 3

10,000 employees are screened, and 200 have diabetes. Find the prevalence.

Show Steps
Formula: Prevalence = (Existing cases ÷ Examined population) × 100

Step 1: Numerator = 200 (diabetic employees)
Step 2: Denominator = 10,000 (total screened)
Step 3: Calculate: (200 ÷ 10,000) × 100
✅ Answer: 2%
Scenario 4

Among the 9,800 healthy employees, 49 develop diabetes over a year. Find the incidence.

Show Steps
Formula: Incidence = (New cases ÷ At-risk population) × Constant

Step 1: Numerator = 49 (newly diabetic)
Step 2: Denominator = 9,800 (healthy / at-risk only)
Step 3: Calculate: (49 ÷ 9,800) × 1,000
✅ Answer: 5 per 1,000

Exam Traps

Common pitfalls and trick questions to watch out for

High-Yield Exam Traps — Don't Fall For These!
  • The incidence denominator MUST strictly exclude previously diagnosed cases (at-risk only).
  • Improved diagnostic tools falsely raise prevalence by finding previously hidden cases.
  • Excellent chronic disease treatments prolong life without curing, heavily increasing prevalence.
  • Cross-sectional studies provide point prevalence, never true longitudinal incidence.
  • Out-migration of healthy people paradoxically increases prevalence by shrinking the denominator.
  • In-migration of susceptible people directly increases the measured disease prevalence.
  • An extremely high case-fatality rate rapidly decreases point prevalence.
  • Better disease reporting systems create an artificial spike in calculated incidence.
